Marginal relief is a built-in safeguard in the Income Tax Act that ensures a taxpayer does not end up paying more *additional* tax than the *additional* income earned above a particular threshold. This guide explains what it means, how surcharge works, who can claim it, and how it is calculated — with examples for FY 2025-26 and FY 2026-27.

<h2 id="mr-1">What is Marginal Relief?</h2>

Marginal relief ensures that when your income crosses a threshold such as ₹50 lakh or ₹1 crore by a small amount, the extra tax liability (including surcharge) never exceeds the extra income earned above that threshold. Without it, the additional tax could be larger than the additional income itself.

The relief is calculated as the difference between the excess tax payable (including surcharge) and the excess income over the applicable threshold.

<h2 id="mr-2">Why Marginal Relief Exists</h2>

India follows a progressive tax system, and a surcharge is charged on top of income tax once income crosses specified limits. A small increase in income can trigger a full surcharge on the entire tax amount, creating an unfairly steep jump in liability. For instance, a person earning ₹49.9 lakh pays no surcharge, but the moment income crosses ₹50 lakh, a 10% surcharge applies to the whole tax liability. Marginal relief exists as a corrective mechanism so this sudden jump does not penalise taxpayers whose income only marginally exceeds a threshold.

<h2 id="mr-3">Understanding Surcharge on Income Tax</h2>

A surcharge is not a separate tax on income — it is an additional charge levied on the income tax amount once total income crosses specified limits. A common misconception is that surcharge applies to the income itself; in reality, it applies only to the calculated tax. After the surcharge, a 4% Health and Education Cess is added on the total of income tax plus surcharge.

<h2 id="mr-4">Surcharge Rates for FY 2025-26 & FY 2026-27</h2>

Budget 2026 retained the surcharge structure carried forward from earlier years. For individuals, HUFs and similar taxpayers, the rates are:

| Total Income | Old Regime | New Regime |
|---|---|---|
| Up to ₹50 lakh | Nil | Nil |
| Above ₹50 lakh – ₹1 crore | 10% | 10% |
| Above ₹1 crore – ₹2 crore | 15% | 15% |
| Above ₹2 crore – ₹5 crore | 25% | 25% |
| Above ₹5 crore | 37% | 25% (capped) |

> **Note:** Under the new tax regime, the maximum surcharge is capped at 25%, which lowers the maximum effective tax rate compared with the old regime's 37%. The enhanced surcharge of 25% and 37% is not levied on dividend income or certain capital gains — surcharge on such income is capped at 15%.

<h2 id="mr-5">Two Types of Marginal Relief</h2>

Marginal relief operates in two distinct situations:

**a) Marginal relief on surcharge** — This applies to high-income taxpayers whose income crosses the surcharge thresholds of ₹50 lakh, ₹1 crore, ₹2 crore or ₹5 crore by a small margin. It ensures the extra tax (including surcharge) does not exceed the income earned above the nearest threshold. Available in both old and new regimes.

**b) Marginal relief on the Section 87A rebate (new regime)** — Under the new tax regime for FY 2025-26 and FY 2026-27, income up to ₹12 lakh is effectively tax-free due to the Section 87A rebate (₹12.75 lakh for salaried individuals after the standard deduction). Marginal relief ensures a taxpayer whose income only slightly exceeds ₹12 lakh does not pay a disproportionately high tax.

<h2 id="mr-6">Who Can Claim Marginal Relief?</h2>

Marginal relief can be claimed by all persons for whom a surcharge becomes applicable — resident and non-resident individuals, HUFs, firms, and companies. For surcharge purposes it is available under both regimes. The rebate-related marginal relief under the new regime is available only to resident individuals within the specified income band. It is applied automatically during tax computation and does not need to be separately claimed.

<h2 id="mr-7">How to Calculate Marginal Relief</h2>

The general formula is:

> **Marginal Relief = (Tax payable including surcharge on actual income) − (Tax on the threshold income + Income exceeding the threshold)**

In practice, you compare the additional tax caused by the surcharge with the additional income earned above the threshold. If the additional tax is higher, the difference is granted as marginal relief.

<h2 id="mr-8">Example: Income Above ₹50 Lakh</h2>

Suppose an individual has a total income of **₹51 lakh** in a financial year. Since income exceeds ₹50 lakh, a 10% surcharge applies on the computed tax. Without relief, for earning an extra ₹1 lakh above ₹50 lakh, the additional tax works out to more than ₹1 lakh — clearly disproportionate.

Marginal relief steps in so the extra tax (including surcharge) does not exceed the ₹1 lakh of additional income. The excess is granted as marginal relief, reducing the final liability. A 4% Health and Education Cess then applies on the reduced amount.

The same principle applies at the ₹1 crore, ₹2 crore and ₹5 crore thresholds.

<h2 id="mr-9">Example: Marginal Relief on the ₹12 Lakh Rebate (New Regime)</h2>

Under the new regime, income up to ₹12 lakh attracts no tax due to the Section 87A rebate. Suppose someone earns slightly more, say ₹12,05,000. Without marginal relief, they could lose the entire rebate and pay tax on the full amount — meaning a person earning less could end up with more money in hand. Marginal relief ensures the tax payable does not exceed the income earned above ₹12 lakh.

<h2 id="mr-10">Marginal Relief for Companies</h2>

For domestic companies, a surcharge of 7% applies where income exceeds ₹1 crore but not ₹10 crore, and 12% where income exceeds ₹10 crore. Marginal relief ensures the total of income tax and surcharge on income above ₹10 crore does not exceed the tax payable on ₹10 crore by more than the amount of income exceeding ₹10 crore. Similar relief applies at the ₹1 crore threshold and to foreign companies at their applicable rates.

<h2 id="mr-11">Key Points to Remember</h2>

- Marginal relief prevents the tax jump from being larger than the income jump above a threshold.
- It applies at every surcharge threshold — ₹50 lakh, ₹1 crore, ₹2 crore and ₹5 crore.
- It is available in both the old and new tax regimes for surcharge purposes.
- A separate marginal relief applies on the Section 87A rebate under the new regime around the ₹12 lakh mark.
- Health and Education Cess of 4% is calculated after marginal relief is applied.
- It is part of the computation mechanism and is applied automatically — no separate claim form is required.

<h2 id="mr-12">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

**Q1. Is marginal relief available in the new tax regime?**
Yes. Marginal relief on surcharge is available in both regimes, and an additional marginal relief on the Section 87A rebate applies under the new regime.

**Q2. Do I need to apply separately for marginal relief?**
No. It is built into the tax computation and applied automatically once your income crosses the relevant threshold.

**Q3. At what income does surcharge first apply for individuals?**
Surcharge applies once total income exceeds ₹50 lakh.

**Q4. What is the maximum surcharge under the new regime?**
The surcharge is capped at 25% under the new regime, compared with a maximum of 37% under the old regime.

**Q5. Is cess added before or after marginal relief?**
The 4% Health and Education Cess is added after marginal relief has been applied to the tax and surcharge.
